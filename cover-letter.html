Dear Hiring Committee,

The mandate behind this role is not simply to run factories. It is to create a manufacturing system that can expand capacity, absorb technology transitions, protect customer commitments, and improve economics without turning every site into a copy of every other site.

That is the operating problem I have prepared to address. My career connects advanced electronics and high-reliability manufacturing at Compunetics, 24/7 customer-backward operations at Amazon, robotics and machine-vision operations at Vape-Jet, regulated autonomous systems at ZeusVu, and materials science and physical chemistry grounding from the University of Pittsburgh. Across those environments, the recurring work has been the same: make complex work visible, clarify ownership, build operating mechanisms, and turn technical and business signals into disciplined execution.

My thesis for this position is straightforward: standardize the operating contract; differentiate the technical capability. A global manufacturing organization needs one enterprise cadence for safety, quality, delivery, capacity, capital, escalation, and learning. It also needs the judgment to protect legitimate process differences, technical centers of excellence, and local expertise that create customer value.

I built an independent candidate vision for the role at https://russelldudek.github.io/VPConfidential/. It includes the Manufacturing Shape Contract, an interactive scenario model, an executive scorecard, a hard-objection analysis, and a first-120-day entry plan. The work is intentionally framed as a hypothesis for discovery because the employer is confidential; it does not pretend to know undisclosed company facts.

The value I would bring is not a prepackaged reorganization. It is a disciplined way to read the system, expose the few constraints that matter most, align decision rights, and prove one consequential cross-site mechanism before scaling change.

I would welcome the opportunity to discuss the company moment, the manufacturing decisions this leader must make in year one, and the operating evidence the leadership team needs to move with confidence.

Carpe diem,

Russell Dudek
