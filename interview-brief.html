Interview thesis brief

Change the shape. Hold the promise.

An evidence-grounded hypothesis for leading a complex global manufacturing network through growth, technology transition, and operating pressure.

Actual mandate

Turn a diverse manufacturing footprint into one enterprise system that can scale capacity, improve economics, support NPI and technology transitions, and protect customer commitments without erasing legitimate local technical capability.

Company moment - discovery hypothesis

Growth raises the cost of ambiguity

Capacity, capital, make-buy, and site roles must become enterprise decisions rather than disconnected local optimizations.

Technical transitions raise execution risk

NPI and process transfer must integrate engineering, quality, operations, supply chain, talent, and customer readiness.

Margin pressure exposes operating variation

The challenge is to remove non-value variation while protecting differences that create technical advantage.

Global scale raises leadership burden

A common operating cadence succeeds only when site leaders retain clear authority and the bench can carry change.

White-space thesis

Standardize the operating contract. Differentiate the technical capability. Define the customer promise, capability map, capacity logic, and control system so the network can change shape with evidence.

The operating tensions

The role succeeds by managing contradictions, not by choosing one side permanently.

Global standards / local judgment

Standardize performance language, decisions, escalation, and learning. Preserve the process knowledge closest to the work.

Delivery now / capacity later

Protect current commitments while building the options required for future demand and product transitions.

Utilization / resilience

Use asset economics without creating a brittle network that fails under demand or supply disruption.

NPI speed / process stability

Move technology quickly only when capability, quality, training, and recovery evidence are explicit.

Automation / adoption

Attach digital and automation investments to changed work, retained human authority, training, and measurable operating results.

Enterprise ambition / site capacity

Sequence change so leaders and frontline teams can absorb it without degrading execution.

Executive questions and first proof
Questions
  1. Where does the manufacturing network most often break the customer promise?
  2. Which site differences are strategic capabilities, and which are historical variation?
  3. What capital or footprint decision must be made in the first year?
  4. How are NPI readiness and technology transfer governed today?
  5. Which two metrics are trusted across every site, and which are debated?
  6. Where is make-versus-buy logic clear, and where is it driven by precedent?
  7. What leadership bench risk would concern the CEO most?
  8. What must be measurably different twelve months after this hire?
First consequential proof

Run one live decision through the Manufacturing Shape Contract

  • Choose a real capacity, NPI, technology transfer, or footprint question.
  • Define the customer promise and non-negotiable constraints.
  • Map capabilities and legitimate site differentiation.
  • Compare capacity, make-buy, capital, and timing options.
  • Clarify enterprise, site, business, engineering, and quality authority.
  • Document the decision, assumptions, risks, leading indicators, and review date.
External context

Public industry sources point to simultaneous capacity expansion, advanced-technology investment, automation, cybersecurity, regulated manufacturing, and workforce capability pressure. These are industry context only; company-specific implications remain hypotheses for discovery.
